## What you need

A bowl of cold water, or a cold wet washcloth. Cold tap water (around 50°F / 10°C) is enough. Ice water is not necessary and is not safer.

## What to do

1. Breathe normally for a few seconds. Don't hyperventilate first.
2. Lean forward over the bowl and submerge your face from forehead to chin in the cold water. Stay submerged for fifteen to thirty seconds.
3. Come up. Breathe normally. Notice the slower heart rate and the shift in alertness.

If a bowl isn't available, press a cold wet washcloth firmly over the eyes, forehead, and cheeks for the same duration. The reflex is strongest when the area around the eyes and nose is cooled.

Repeat once or twice if needed. Avoid more than three immersions in a short window.

## Why it works

Cold water on the face engages the mammalian dive reflex: heart rate drops within seconds, blood vessels in the limbs constrict, and the parasympathetic branch of the nervous system activates. This is the same reflex that helps marine mammals stay underwater for extended periods.

The effect is faster and stronger than breath-based practices. It is one of the few interventions that can interrupt an acute panic or rage state in under a minute. The cold doesn't need to be intense; ordinary tap-cold water works for most people.

The cold face response is also used clinically in cardiology to terminate certain types of fast heart rhythms, and forms part of dialectical behavior therapy's TIP skill for acute distress tolerance.
